How do Satellites Stay in orbit?

Well this is a question which most of the people new to astronomy and astrophysics have, HOW does a space probe stay in orbit around a celestial body without having constant propulsion.

The answer to this, is a very simple one, which needs nothing more than a basic hang of centrifugal (or pseudo) force and gravitational force.

Balance??

So basically, the velocity of the probe and the gravitational pull of the celestial body it orbits, determines the ability of the probe to stay in orbit

The closer the probe is, to the celestial body, the faster it has to travel to maintain it's orbit (well not necessarily if the celestial body's gravitational force is low/negligible)

What about satellites on Earth?

To avoid incertitude and confusion, lets take the example of our home planet, Earth

Normally, the lowest orbit at which a probe can revolve (considering that the probe has enough velocity to counter the g-force) around Earth is Low Earth Orbit (LEO) or often called Parking Orbit, as satellites temporarily orbi at that orbit before it performs a anti-gravity boost which takes it to a higher, and safer orbit.

But we must know that this is altogether a game of balance, as a small change in it's velocity/centrifugal pseudo force can lead to it falling back to earth in a gradual manner, or drifting OUT of earth's gravitational field
